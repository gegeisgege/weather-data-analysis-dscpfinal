Project Overview

This exploratory data analysis examines weather and air quality data from monitoring stations across Taiwan, focusing on pollution patterns and their relationships with meteorological conditions.

Temporal Analysis

Identify hourly and daily patterns in air pollutant concentrations including PM2.5, PM10, NO2, and CO across different time periods.

Correlation Study

Investigate relationships between meteorological variables (temperature, humidity) and air quality measurements.

Spatial Comparison

Compare air quality levels across multiple monitoring stations to identify geographic patterns and hotspots.

Health Insights

Assess public health implications by analyzing exceedances of air quality standards and identifying high-risk periods.

Key Findings

Primary insights derived from the analysis of weather and air quality data.

Diurnal Pollution Cycles

PM2.5 concentrations exhibit clear daily patterns with peaks during morning (7-9 AM) and evening (6-8 PM) rush hours, suggesting vehicular traffic as a primary contributor to urban air pollution.

Temperature-Pollution Relationship

A negative correlation exists between ambient temperature and PM2.5 levels, indicating that higher temperatures facilitate better atmospheric dispersion and mixing of pollutants.

Geographic Variability

Significant differences in air quality observed across monitoring stations, with urban locations consistently showing higher pollutant concentrations compared to rural or coastal areas.

Health Standard Exceedances

Analysis reveals that a notable percentage of measurement periods exceed WHO air quality guidelines (PM2.5 > 35 μg/m³), highlighting potential public health concerns.

Multi-Pollutant Correlations

Strong positive correlations identified between related pollutants (PM2.5 and PM10, nitrogen oxides), while meteorological variables show weaker but significant relationships.
